Whole-child focus
Instead of: Technical and applied CS skills with little attention to holistic well-being
Students develop CS and AI knowledge through kinesthetic activities, build emotional and social skills through digital citizenship and self-reflection, and experience safety, comfort, and privacy in online and in-person environments.
Connection & community
Instead of: Individual practice and competition
Students collaborate frequently with peers and community on CS challenges — coding projects, design challenges, community-based tech solutions. Every learner's identity is a strength. Perspectives are accepted, respected, and celebrated, especially across different ability levels.
High expectations & rigor
Instead of: Limited access for a select few, rote tasks, memorization
Every student — regardless of background or prior exposure — accesses high-quality CS and AI learning at grade level. Tasks demand rigorous thinking: analyzing data, recognizing patterns, synthesizing knowledge, and applying it in real-world contexts while maintaining critical judgment of AI outputs.
Relevance
Instead of: Standardized, hypothetical scenarios that "prepare" students for later
Students engage in meaningful tasks that build on their interests, use cutting-edge technology including AI, and connect to real-world problems now. Materials reflect their backgrounds, cultures, and communities. They experience how human creativity and technology shape current careers and society.
Customization
Instead of: Standardized instruction regardless of need
Learning structures are flexible — how, when, where, and from whom students engage is based on what supports them best. Content is tailored to individual needs, interests, and goals. AI and computing tools provide scaffolds that get students unstuck faster. Multiple pathways exist to demonstrate mastery.
Agency
Instead of: Passive absorption of content
Students take ownership of their CS and AI learning — choosing how they learn, making personal connections, exploring technology's impact on society, and building socially responsible solutions. They document and share outputs, contribute to classroom and community dialogue, and take action to positively impact their communities.

Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Foundational computational thinking, block-based programming, unplugged activities, and early AI concepts — classification, patterns, fairness. Strong emphasis on orientation and modeling.
Middle School (6–8)
Programming constructs, data representation, structured practice. AI learning includes simple model-building and evaluation, with reflection on limitations.
High School (9–12)
Complex systems, data pipelines, responsible AI use. Sustained projects with explicit feedback. Pathways into careers and postsecondary CS.
