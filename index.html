Computational Literacy · Design Guidance

Reimagining Computer Science & AI learning

Today's students are entering a world where digital systems, artificial intelligence, and computational thinking shape nearly every aspect of life. Yet most K–12 computer science experiences remain narrowly focused on basic skills in ways that are disconnected from students' interests and futures.

The challenge before schools is not simply to "add CS and AI" to existing programs. It is to redesign learning so that young people become capable, ethical, creative, and empowered participants in an AI-enabled world. Federal and state investments are growing, public support is strong, and the research on effective CS learning is sharpening. This is a rare alignment of opportunity communities can't afford to miss.

Meeting this moment calls for a shared definition of rigorous outcomes and a coherent redesign of how CS and AI are learned — which is what this framework provides.

The student experience

Extraordinary CS and AI learning requires six leaps away from industrial-era models, and three types of experience — core instruction, enrichment, and intervention — working together so every student is challenged, supported, and motivated.

Six leaps toward extraordinary learning

Whole-child focus
CS learning that builds identity, wellbeing, and belonging alongside technical skills
Connection & community
Collaborative computing — pair programming, community problems, shared CS culture
High expectations & rigor
Grade-level CS and AI access for every student, with rigorous thinking at the center
Relevance
Real problems, real tools, real connections to students' lives and communities
Customization
Flexible pathways, scaffolded support, and multiple ways to show CS mastery
Agency
Students as creators and problem-solvers — shaping technology, not just using it
Whole-child focus
Instead of: Technical and applied CS skills with little attention to holistic well-being
Students develop CS and AI knowledge through kinesthetic activities, build emotional and social skills through digital citizenship and self-reflection, and experience safety, comfort, and privacy in online and in-person environments.
Connection & community
Instead of: Individual practice and competition
Students collaborate frequently with peers and community on CS challenges — coding projects, design challenges, community-based tech solutions. Every learner's identity is a strength. Perspectives are accepted, respected, and celebrated, especially across different ability levels.
High expectations & rigor
Instead of: Limited access for a select few, rote tasks, memorization
Every student — regardless of background or prior exposure — accesses high-quality CS and AI learning at grade level. Tasks demand rigorous thinking: analyzing data, recognizing patterns, synthesizing knowledge, and applying it in real-world contexts while maintaining critical judgment of AI outputs.
Relevance
Instead of: Standardized, hypothetical scenarios that "prepare" students for later
Students engage in meaningful tasks that build on their interests, use cutting-edge technology including AI, and connect to real-world problems now. Materials reflect their backgrounds, cultures, and communities. They experience how human creativity and technology shape current careers and society.
Customization
Instead of: Standardized instruction regardless of need
Learning structures are flexible — how, when, where, and from whom students engage is based on what supports them best. Content is tailored to individual needs, interests, and goals. AI and computing tools provide scaffolds that get students unstuck faster. Multiple pathways exist to demonstrate mastery.
Agency
Instead of: Passive absorption of content
Students take ownership of their CS and AI learning — choosing how they learn, making personal connections, exploring technology's impact on society, and building socially responsible solutions. They document and share outputs, contribute to classroom and community dialogue, and take action to positively impact their communities.

Parts of the student experience

Core Instruction
The primary grade-level experience for all students. Establishes the foundational knowledge and skills that enrichment and intervention build from.
Enrichment
Raises the ceiling — extending learning through deeper complexity, creativity, and authentic problem-solving that builds computing identity and motivation.
Intervention
Acts as a safety net — providing targeted additional support without replacing access to grade-level learning.
School elements

Every element of the school must be intentionally designed to shape the student experience and drive these outcomes.

The opportunity

Today's students are entering a world where digital systems, artificial intelligence, and computational thinking shape nearly every aspect of life. Yet most K–12 computer science experiences remain narrowly focused on basic skills in ways that are disconnected from students' interests and futures.

The challenge before schools is not simply to "add CS and AI" to existing programs. It is to redesign learning so that young people can become capable, ethical, creative, and empowered participants in an AI-enabled world. The need for this has never been more urgent — or more achievable. Federal and state investments are growing. Public support is strong. Research on how students learn CS most effectively is sharpening. And the policy momentum is real. This is a rare alignment of opportunity that communities can't afford to miss. Explore these trends and more →

Where we stand
The opportunities are real
  • Coordinated federal and state investment in CS and AI education — $88M+ in state budgets in 2024 alone
  • 11 states now require CS credits for high school graduation
  • 20 state departments of education have issued official AI guidance for K–12
  • 65% of caregivers agree schools should teach students how to use AI
  • 80%+ of teachers, caregivers, and students say AI has had a positive impact on education
  • Growing research consensus on what effective CS and AI learning looks like
  • CS and AI increasingly treated as connected disciplines rather than separate silos
The challenges are genuine
  • Incomplete standards frameworks that combine CS and AI Literacy comprehensively
  • AI advancing faster than pedagogical knowledge and teacher preparation systems
  • CS lacks core subject status in most states — finding dedicated time is hard
  • Limited evidence of curriculum effectiveness makes informed choices difficult
  • Persistent access disparities by race, gender, geography, and school size
  • Only 48% of teachers feel equipped to teach AI; 88% need more PD resources
  • Rapid proliferation of AI tools without quality control or evaluation frameworks

Public & Professional Will toward CS Education
Educators, caregivers, and students all recognize the importance and value of computer science.
Show ↓
80% of principals, 74% of superintendents, and 70% of teachers "strongly agree" or "agree" that people who do computer science make things that help improve people's lives. Gallup, 2020
69% of parents see strong value in CS for societal impact and career opportunities. Parents of Black students see more value (78%) than parents of Hispanic (67%) or white students (68%). Gallup, 2020
About 40% of all students see learning computer science as important for them — including 42% of Black students, 40% of Hispanic students, and 39% of white students. Gallup, 2020
Students exposed to 5+ hours of CS per week are nearly 3x more likely to see it as very important (69%) compared to those with zero exposure (26%). Gallup, 2020
Computer science is among the top 4 subjects for both importance and favorite subject. Nearly 3-in-4 students view it as important, with two-thirds saying it will become more important in coming years. CSforAll, 2025
02
K–12 AI Education
CS teachers strongly support AI integration and request guidance; students recognize AI's importance but feel underprepared.
Show ↓
85% of CS teachers believe using and learning about AI should be included in foundational CS experiences. 80% agree that CS standards should include AI. CSTA and Teach AI, 2024
74% of students (ages 12–17 across Europe) believe AI will play a significant role in their professional lives — but only 46% think their schools adequately prepare them for AI, and 49% worry AI could widen gaps in academic success. European Commission, OECD & Code.org, 2025
76% of teachers agree AI can promote creativity in the classroom. CS teachers most commonly cite increasing learning opportunities, automating differentiation tasks, and preparing students for the digital world as key benefits. CSTA and Teach AI, 2024
"Learning computer science is still crucial for students, even with the rise of AI. Understanding the principles behind coding and computational thinking not only enables students to create AI but also helps them grasp its limitations, ethical considerations, and potential social impacts."— Secondary CS Teacher, New York, USA (CSTA and Teach AI, 2024)
03
Access, Participation & Disparities in CS Education
CS enrollment is growing — but access gaps by race, gender, and geography persist and demand attention.
Show ↓
High school CS enrollment grew from 5.1% of students in 2020–21 to 6.4% in 2023–24 — a 25% increase. States with at least six of Code.org's recommended policies average 70% of students taking foundational CS courses. Code.org, 2024
State adoption of CS graduation requirements jumped 37.5% in 2024, rising from 8 to 11 states. State funding for CS education has grown from 9 to 39 states since 2017. Code.org, 2024
White and Asian students comprise 58% of CS participants while Black and Hispanic students remain underrepresented relative to their population sizes. Female enrollment has risen slightly but males still represent 67.5% of enrollment vs. 32.5% for females. Code.org, 2024
Students from lower-income families, rural schools, and smaller schools have significantly less access. School size gaps are stark: 36% of the smallest schools offer CS vs. 93% of the largest. Code.org, 2024
04
CS Outcomes & Disparities
Tech careers offer exceptional growth and pay — but achievement gaps at the AP level signal who currently has access to the pipeline.
Show ↓
Tech salaries are about 127% higher than the national 50th percentile. Tech occupation employment over the next 10 years is expected to grow at roughly twice the rate of overall employment — with Data Scientists projected to grow 414%. CompTIA, 2025
AP Computer Science Principles shows racial gaps: Asian students pass at 78%, white students at 69%, Black students at 33%, and Native Hawaiian students at 40% — despite the course being designed to broaden participation. Code.org, 2024
AP Computer Science A: Asian students achieve passing scores at more than double the rate of Black students (75% vs. 36%). Males outperform females (68% vs. 63%). Code.org, 2024
05
Structural & Systemic Barriers to CS Education
Funding and teacher preparation are the most-cited levers — and the most underdeveloped.
Show ↓
School leaders cite funding (34–36%) and career exposure (23–30%) as the top things businesses could do to increase access to high-quality CS education. Gallup, 2020
Only 48% of teachers currently feel equipped to teach AI. 88% need more professional learning resources. The most requested PD topics: how AI works, how to use AI tools, and the ethical impacts of AI. CSTA and Teach AI, 2024
The field lacks vetted, comprehensive K–12 AI curricula aligned with emerging standards. Most available AI resources are short modules rather than comprehensive curricula. No established rubrics exist for evaluating pedagogical quality, alignment, or equity features. Code.org, 2024
06
Future Trends & Emerging Innovations in CS Education
Teachers are optimistic about AI in CS — and advocates for curriculum that evolves without abandoning fundamentals.
Show ↓
52% of CS teachers see AI's benefits as outweighing its risks; 43% see equal benefits and risks. Top concerns: overreliance, plagiarism, and widening access gaps. CSTA and Teach AI, 2024
Teachers advocate for curriculum evolution that maintains fundamental programming skills while incorporating AI-relevant topics and societal impact discussions in foundational CS courses. CSTA and Teach AI, 2024
Texas leads the nation in tech job creation (8,181 new positions) with the second-largest tech workforce (972,747 jobs). Dallas ranks #2 nationally in absolute tech employment (378,945 jobs); Austin's tech sector represents 22% of its local economy (#4 nationally). CompTIA, 2025
Foundational CS access in Texas increased to 56% of public high schools by 2023 — but gaps persist by geography (50–73%) and school size (36–93%). Male students participate at nearly a 3:1 ratio compared to females (72% vs. 28%). Code.org, 2024
Call to action

Computer science is no longer a niche discipline reserved for a select few careers. It is a foundational literacy — a new medium for thinking, designing, creating, and solving problems across every domain of human life. And importantly, it includes the nascent study and use of artificial intelligence.

Meeting this moment requires more than adding a coding class. It calls for a shared definition of rigorous outcomes and a coherent redesign of CS and AI learning experiences — redesigning outcomes, the student experience, and the school elements that make both possible.

1
Foundations of Artificial Intelligence Literacy
Knowledge and skills to understand what AI is, how it works, recognize its presence and influence, evaluate AI outputs critically, and distinguish between human and AI capabilities.
Why this domain matters
AI systems now shape decisions in hiring, healthcare, education, and civic life. Students who cannot recognize AI, evaluate its outputs, or understand its limitations are not positioned to participate fully as citizens or workers. This domain builds the foundational literacy every student needs — regardless of whether they pursue computing as a career.

2
Computational Thinking
Knowledge and skills to collaboratively approach complex problems systematically — breaking them down, identifying patterns, extracting essential information, developing step-by-step solutions, and analyzing data to drive informed decisions.
Why this domain matters
Computational thinking is the problem-solving engine underneath all of CS and AI. Research shows it transfers across disciplines — students with strong CT skills approach challenges in science, math, and humanities more effectively. It is the rigorous technical foundation that makes the other domains meaningful.

It is the domain most associated with the CS career pipeline, and also where access inequities are sharpest. Rigorous, high-quality CS education for every student is a matter of equity.

Whole-child focus
Industrial-era learning
Narrow Focus

Learners engage mostly in academic learning activities that focus on technical and applied computer science skills with little attention to the development of their holistic well-being.

Extraordinary learning
Whole-Child Focus
  • Development of computer science and AI knowledge and skills through kinesthetic activities, such as modeling or building hardware.
  • Development of emotional and social skills through digital literacy and citizenship instruction, self-reflection, and activities that promote self-regulation with technology use.
  • Safety, comfort, and privacy in online and in-person environments, both in and out of class.
Connection & community
Industrial-era learning
Isolation & Conformity

Learners develop computer science and computing skills primarily through individual, independent practice, and are often encouraged to compete and outperform one another.

Extraordinary learning
Connection & Community
  • Many opportunities to communicate and collaborate with peers through academic and community-building activities like coding projects, design challenges, or community-based tech solutions that promote teamwork, shared goals, and appreciation of diverse problem-solving approaches.
  • Acceptance, respect, and celebration for who they are, their perspectives, and their contributions in computer science and AI learning, especially when working with others of different ability levels.
  • Opportunities to learn when and how humans, AI, or computer programs can best assist or complete tasks.
High expectations & rigor
Industrial-era learning
Low Expectations with Surface-Level Learning

Access to computer science is often limited to a select few, and learning tends to focus narrowly on rote hardware, software, or coding tasks, emphasizing memorization over deep understanding.

Extraordinary learning
High Expectations with Rigorous Learning
  • The same access to computer science and AI learning, regardless of their background or prior exposure.
  • Content and activities that are aligned with or exceed grade-level expectations, offering the appropriate level of support and challenge to grow their skills.
  • Computer science and AI tasks that demand rigorous thinking — analyzing data and recognizing patterns, synthesizing, and applying knowledge in real-world contexts while skillfully communicating with AI and maintaining critical judgment to evaluate outputs against their own voice and values.
  • Activities that build factual, conceptual, and procedural knowledge of computer science and AI, while also fostering awareness of multiple perspectives in the design and impact of technology.
Relevance
Industrial-era learning
Irrelevance

Computer science knowledge and skills are learned through hypothetical, standardized, and often uninteresting activities and scenarios to prepare young people for meaningful and authentic work later in life.

Extraordinary learning
Relevance
  • Opportunities to amplify students' strengths using computer science and AI while also getting targeted help and freedom to focus on creative and personally meaningful work.
  • Computer science and AI materials and assignments that reflect their backgrounds and cultures, as well as real-life data sets that connect to students' lives and communities.
  • Tasks that intentionally build on their prior knowledge.
  • Opportunities to use computer science and AI to bring ideas to life and experience how human creativity and technology work together, enable current careers, and shape society.
Customization
Industrial-era learning
One-Size-Fits-All Approach

All learners who can access computer science courses receive the same standardized instruction, regardless of specific needs.

Extraordinary learning
Customization
  • Learning structures that enable flexibility in how, when, where, and from whom they engage in computer science and AI, based on what supports them best.
  • Content that is tailored to their individual needs, interests, and learning goals, enabled by AI programs or computer-based software where possible.
  • Scaffolds and responsive supports to navigate complex computer science and AI learning successfully, such as AI that gets them unstuck faster so they can keep moving forward in their learning.
  • Multiple, flexible opportunities to demonstrate mastery of computer science and AI learning.
Agency
Industrial-era learning
Passive Compliance

Learners passively absorb computer science content from teachers or digital platforms, with minimal opportunities to explore ethical implications, make personal connections, engage with diverse perspectives, or solve pressing real-world problems.

Extraordinary learning
Agency
  • Opportunities to tackle ambitious challenges by making choices about when and how to use, modify, or build AI and computer programs that help them access information, overcome obstacles, and iterate faster.
  • Students routinely share and document their outputs with others to showcase their learning and contribute to classroom or community dialogue around safe and innovative uses of technology.
  • Opportunities to take individual or collective action, such as designing technologies or solutions using computer programs or AI, to positively impact their communities and the world.

Core Instruction
Enrichment
Intervention
What it is
Core instruction establishes the foundation for learning in Computational Literacy and AI. It is the primary, grade-level learning experience that all students participate in and is designed to meet the needs of the vast majority of learners.
Why it matters
When core instruction is strong and delivered at grade level, more students gain access to rigorous computing learning without being tracked out of opportunity. Because computing learning is cumulative, core instruction is essential for establishing the foundational knowledge students need to succeed over time. Seemingly small gaps — misunderstandings about variables, conditionals, functions, or data structures — can quickly block future learning. Core instruction also builds the shared language and practices that interventions and enrichment rely on, such as debugging routines, code readability expectations, and responsible AI decision-making.
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Foundational computational thinking, block-based programming, unplugged activities, and early AI concepts — classification, patterns, fairness. Strong emphasis on orientation and modeling. Offered as a core class or integrated into STEM.
Middle School (6–8)
Increased focus on programming constructs, data representation, and structured practice. AI learning includes simple model-building and evaluation with reflection on limitations. Offered as a core class or integrated into STEM.
High School (9–12)
More complex systems, data pipelines, and responsible AI use. Core instruction supports sustained projects while preserving explicit feedback and reflection. Offered as a core class, integrated into STEM, or through defined pathways.
The four-phase learning cycle in core instruction
① Orientation to Learning
  • Clarifies learning goals, success criteria, and relevance — why a programming concept or AI system matters in the real world.
  • Establishes a belief that all students can learn Computational Literacy and AI, countering stereotypes about who belongs in computing.
  • Connects new learning to prior knowledge and future applications.
  • All students are expected to master the same standards, though the exact sequence and timeline may differ from student to student.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Introduces new concepts and skills through explicit modeling, worked examples, and developmentally appropriate inquiry.
  • Uses various approaches — visuals, code walkthroughs, discussion, simulations — to reduce cognitive load.
  • Balances direct instruction with guided exploration as students build initial mental models.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Provides frequent, scaffolded opportunities to apply learning — debugging routines, short builds, data explorations.
  • Includes both deliberate practice for fluency and authentic tasks for application.
  • Encourages collaboration (e.g., pair programming) when it supports focus and learning goals.
  • Students' outputs or how they show mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Provides timely, specific feedback aligned with clear criteria — code quality, reasoning, process, and responsible use.
  • Includes opportunities to revise work and reflect on strategies used.
  • Builds student self-efficacy by making progress visible.
The distinction between core, intervention, and enrichment is not whether students engage in these types of learning — but rather the level of support, intensity, time, and complexity each requires. All three parts share the same four-phase learning cycle: Orientation to Learning → Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills → Practice & Demonstration → Feedback & Reflection. What it is
Enrichment raises the ceiling — extending learning beyond the core experience by increasing depth, complexity, creativity, and authenticity. It provides opportunities for students to explore Computational Literacy in more open-ended ways while building identity, motivation, and a sense of agency as capable problem-solvers and creators.
Why it matters
Enrichment helps students deepen understanding and transfer core skills into new contexts, strengthening long-term learning and flexible problem-solving. In Computational Literacy and AI, enrichment is a powerful driver of identity development: students are more likely to persist in computing pathways when they experience autonomy, meaningful purpose, and opportunities to create work that matters to them or their communities. Enrichment creates space for curiosity, innovation, and challenge — helping students move beyond "getting the code to work" toward designing, refining, and communicating solutions. It also supports responsible AI literacy by giving students structured opportunities to explore how AI systems behave, where they fall short, and what ethical considerations come with using them.
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Enrichment often looks like creative computing — interactive stories, games, animations, or simple AI-inspired sorting and classifying activities tied to student interests and play.
Middle School (6–8)
Students engage in mini-projects with data and AI tools — experimenting with classification, critiquing limitations, refining designs — often in collaborative teams.
High School (9–12)
Enrichment can expand into capstones, advanced electives, community-based projects, research experiences, internships, competitions, or deeper AI systems work with strong ethical and safety guardrails.
The four-phase learning cycle in enrichment
① Orientation to Learning
  • Centers student choice, purpose, and relevance — "What problem do you want to solve with computing or AI?"
  • Connects learning to real-world meaning, community issues, or student interests.
  • Builds motivation and ownership by allowing students to define success and set goals.
  • Students know how their enrichment learning connects to the standards in core instruction.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Introduces advanced or extended content connected to core learning — APIs, new libraries, model evaluation ideas, new data sources.
  • Provides optional scaffolds so students can stretch without getting stuck.
  • Encourages exploration through structured inquiry rather than step-by-step replication.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Emphasizes authentic application through longer builds — projects, prototypes, investigations, or community-based challenges.
  • Expands what "demonstration" can look like: code, design artifacts, testing plans, user research, ethical analyses.
  • Encourages iteration, experimentation, and problem-solving under real constraints.
  • How students demonstrate mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Incorporates feedback from multiple perspectives — peers, teachers, mentors, end users, community members.
  • Emphasizes reflection on both product and process: what changed, why decisions were made, tradeoffs, and impact.
  • Builds metacognition, communication skills, and a deeper computing identity — "I am someone who can build, test, and improve systems."

What it is
Intervention acts as a safety net — providing targeted, additional support for students who are not yet making sufficient progress in core Computational Literacy learning. It is designed to address specific learning needs without replacing access to grade-level instruction.
Why it matters
Computing learning is cumulative, and even small misunderstandings — confusion about variables, Boolean logic, conditionals, loops, functions, or data handling — can quickly become barriers to future learning. Within a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) framework, intervention serves as a responsive layer of support that helps students strengthen foundational skills while staying connected to Tier 1 grade-level goals. High-quality intervention also protects student confidence and persistence: programming often includes frequent "failure states" (bugs, errors, unexpected outputs), and targeted support paired with rapid feedback can rebuild self-efficacy and reduce disengagement.
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Supports rely on concrete and multimodal approaches — unplugged routines, block sequencing cards, guided tool use — to strengthen early logic and sequencing skills.
Middle School (6–8)
Common intervention focuses include debugging routines, conditional reasoning, variable tracking, and understanding code structure through tracing and explanation.
High School (9–12)
Targeted clinics may focus on functions, scope, data structures, testing and debugging workflows, and for AI: dataset quality, evaluation basics, and interpretation of model behavior.
The four-phase learning cycle in intervention
① Orientation to Learning
  • Clarifies the specific skill focus and why it matters — "Today we're working on loops because they help us automate patterns in our programs."
  • Reinforces that growth is expected and achievable, supporting student confidence and persistence.
  • Makes success criteria concrete and visible — "I can trace this code and explain what happens step by step."
  • Students target specific standards based on their unique needs.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Re-introduces key ideas using different representations or more explicit teaching — diagrams, tracing tables, worked examples.
  • Slows pacing and reduces cognitive load by chunking content into smaller steps.
  • Provides guided modeling of thinking processes students may not yet do independently — debugging moves, predicting output, reading error messages.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Focuses practice on a small set of high-leverage tasks — tracing conditionals, fixing one bug type, writing one function pattern.
  • Provides repetition and structured support to build fluency and reduce overwhelm.
  • Uses short, frequent practice cycles that allow students to make progress quickly.
  • How students demonstrate mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Provides rapid, actionable feedback with immediate opportunities to revise and succeed.
  • Helps students name what strategy worked — "I used print statements to locate the bug."
  • Makes progress visible so students can see growth and regain momentum.
Key design principles for intervention
Intervention is additive, not replacement.
Intervention should protect access to grade-level Computational Literacy instruction rather than replace it. Students in intervention still participate in core instruction.
Curriculum alignment is essential.
Intervention tasks should mirror the language, representations, and expectations of core instruction so students aren't learning in a disconnected system.
Staffing matters.
Teachers play a key role in diagnosing needs through student work. Interventionists, specialists, or trained support staff can deliver high-impact small-group sessions using aligned protocols and materials.

School elements design guide

Curriculum, Instruction & Assessment
What students learn, how they learn it, and how learning is measured.
Goals for this design
  • A coordinated, standards-aligned instructional system covering the full scope of Computational Literacy learning outcomes, assembled from multiple curricula and platforms where necessary, with AI integrated throughout and with explicit attention to vertical alignment across grade bands.
  • A set of research-informed instructional principles that guide how Computational Literacy content is taught, acknowledging that AI literacy instruction in particular remains an emerging area where validated pedagogical sequences do not yet exist.
  • Supplemental materials tightly aligned to the core instructional system and adapted for Computational Literacy's distinctive pedagogical needs, selected and sequenced to reinforce the instructional approaches used in core instruction.
  • A differentiated support system providing scaffolded intervention for students who struggle with Computational Literacy concepts, including both programming concepts and AI-specific reasoning skills.
  • A purposeful and multi-layered assessment system that provides actionable data for instructional decision-making, acknowledging that validated Computational Literacy-specific instruments are largely absent.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Curriculum addresses all Computational Literacy learning outcomes with clear progression.
Students build knowledge and skills across grade levels through vetted curricula that develop all six domains — not isolated units, but a coherent K–12 sequence.
Instruction uses varied approaches and activities.
Students learn through explicit instruction, hands-on coding and unplugged activities, collaborative projects, inquiry-based problem-solving, industry experiences, peer discussions, and real-world applications that integrate AI tools and connect to their lives.
Assessment is ongoing and authentic.
Teachers track progress through outcome-aligned exit tickets, project evaluations, peer feedback, self-reflection, class discussions, and diagnostics. Assessments are curriculum-based rather than waiting for standardized tests.
AI literacy is woven throughout instruction.
Students learn how AI works, its applications and limitations, ethical considerations, and how to use AI tools responsibly — embedded throughout the learning sequence, not added on.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Available curricula rarely cover all learning outcomes. Most require stitching multiple resources together, which risks gaps and incoherence.
Design response
Map available curricula against the six outcome domains explicitly. Identify gaps and plan targeted supplementation or custom units for uncovered areas.
Challenge
Traditional CS instruction tends to be isolating rather than collaborative, with students working independently on screens.
Design response
Build in pair programming, group projects, and structured discussions as core instructional strategies. Collaboration is a learning outcome, not a reward.
School & Classroom Culture, Routines & Norms
The shared beliefs, expectations, and patterns of interaction that shape how students experience learning every day.
Goals for this design
  • Computational Literacy classroom behavioral norms explicitly support participation by all students — making it safe to ask questions, attempt problems publicly, share incomplete thinking, and be wrong.
  • Computational Literacy norms are designed around the specific social demands of computing work, including pair programming, collaborative debugging, code review, and peer critique.
  • Behavioral expectations actively challenge the 'geek myth' and other stereotypical cues about who belongs in computing — signaling through environment, norms, and expectations that computing is for everyone.
  • Disciplinary systems protect Computational Literacy learning time rather than removing students from it, with relational and restorative approaches that keep all students in class.
  • Schoolwide behavioral norms are implemented in ways that are explicitly compatible with the kind of engaged, collaborative Computational Literacy instruction that research identifies as essential for learning.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Every student belongs in computing.
Inclusive language, diverse role models, and celebrating varied approaches signal to every student — especially those historically excluded from CS — that they are capable computer scientists and AI users.
Computational Literacy connects to real-world topics students care about.
Computational thinking is grounded in issues students find meaningful — climate change, social justice, community needs, and AI ethics. CS topics that connect the real world to the school community are visualized throughout the school.
Routines build collaboration and digital wellness.
Classroom norms emphasize peer support and productive struggle. Schools promote healthy technology habits, balanced screen time, and mindful use of digital tools including AI.
AI and emerging technologies are ongoing conversations.
Students, families, and staff engage regularly in discussions about AI's impact, ethical computing, and responsible technology use — woven into school culture, not limited to one-time lessons.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS culture often defaults to a narrow "programmer" identity that excludes students who don't self-identify with tech — particularly girls, students of color, and students from under-resourced communities.
Design response
Actively surface diverse computing role models, celebrate multiple ways of solving problems, and connect CS to domains students already care about. Representation in materials and classroom visuals matters.
Challenge
Productive failure — essential in CS — can undermine student confidence if classrooms aren't built to normalize struggle and iteration.
Design response
Build explicit norms around debugging, revision, and learning from errors. Frame "my code doesn't work" as a normal, expected part of the process — not a sign of incapacity.
Schedules & Use of Time
How instructional time is allocated, structured, and protected for CS and AI learning.
Goals for this design
  • Computational Literacy instructional time is protected and recurring — not treated as optional, discretionary, or the first thing cut when other priorities arise.
  • Scheduling decisions ensure universal access: every student receives Computational Literacy instruction regardless of which teacher, schedule track, or pull-out services they are assigned to.
  • Instructional time is designed for depth, not coverage — each session includes sufficient uninterrupted time for students to engage in the full arc of computational work.
  • Students have scheduled time for authentic application and project-based work beyond drill and tutorial, so they develop transferable computational thinking and a computing identity.
  • Intervention or additional support time does not replace access to core Computational Literacy instruction — students receiving supports in other subjects are not systematically excluded.
  • The yearly and unit calendar includes deliberate cycles for formative assessment and responsive regrouping, so instructional time is used adaptively rather than marching through content regardless of evidence of learning.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Adequate time is allocated for Computational Literacy instruction.
Students engage in 2–5 hours per week of Computational Literacy instruction. The master schedule protects this time with the same priority as core subjects.
Computational Literacy is integrated across subjects and schedules.
Computational thinking and AI literacy are woven into math, science, ELA, and social studies — not siloed to a single period. Cross-curricular integration reinforces learning and increases total exposure.
Flexible instructional structures allow for differentiation.
Station rotations, weekly digital citizenship lessons, quarterly Computational Literacy projects, and CS explorations give teachers tools to reach different learners across core instruction, enrichment, and intervention.
Professional learning time is built into the schedule.
Staff schedules include dedicated time for Computational Literacy training, collaborative planning, and ongoing learning about emerging technologies including AI.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS lacks core subject status in most states, making it easy to cut when schedules are tight or competing priorities emerge.
Design response
Anchor CS time to existing schedule structures. Make it non-negotiable at the master schedule level, visible in the school's public communication about what it offers.
Adult Roles, Hiring & Learning
What adults do, what they need to know and believe, how they are hired, and how they are supported to grow.
Goals for this element
  • Educators believe deeply in every student's potential to excel in computing and feel shared responsibility to broaden participation — regardless of background, prior achievement, or whether students see themselves as "tech people."
  • All educators responsible for Computational Literacy instruction possess strong content knowledge and pedagogical expertise, including the ability to anticipate students' thinking and address misconceptions.
  • Collaboration among educators is routine and purposeful — grounded in shared materials and student work, enabling consistent practice across classrooms.
  • Professional learning is ongoing, curriculum-embedded, and directly connected to upcoming instruction — sustained long enough to meaningfully build CS content knowledge and self-efficacy.
  • Educators and leaders understand that Computational Literacy education is a rapidly evolving field, and professional learning systems are designed to keep pace with changes in content, pedagogy, tools, and AI.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Computational Literacy leadership roles are clearly established.
A specialized Computational Literacy teacher with CS expertise, an IT specialist for tech management, and a STEM+Computational Literacy administrator oversee program development. Principals, APs, and instructional coaches actively support Computational Literacy implementation.
Professional development is continuous, curriculum-aligned, and AI-inclusive.
Teachers receive training that builds capacity to understand AI tools, teach AI concepts appropriately, and integrate AI responsibly into Computational Literacy instruction. PD includes coaching cycles, lesson internalization protocols, and PLCs.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Only 48% of teachers currently feel equipped to teach AI, and 88% say they need more professional learning resources. AI advances faster than most PD systems can keep up.
Design response
Build a sustained, multi-year PD system rather than one-time workshops. Partner with curriculum providers for embedded support. Connect educators to professional networks like CSTA.
Challenge
CS educators are often isolated — the only person in their building teaching the subject — which limits collaboration, coaching, and shared improvement.
Design response
Create cross-school or cross-district PLCs for CS educators. Use virtual collaboration tools to connect isolated teammates. Designate a CS instructional lead even in small settings.
Technology & Tech Infrastructure
Devices, connectivity, software, and the systems that keep them working equitably and reliably.
Goals for this design
  • Every student has equitable access to the devices, connectivity, software, and physical materials required to participate fully in Computational Literacy learning, regardless of school resources, home environment, or grade level.
  • The physical classroom environment is intentionally configured to support the mode of computing work students are doing, with furniture, display surfaces, and spatial arrangements that flex between collaborative design work, individual coding, hardware building, and whole-class discussion.
  • Computing tools — devices, software environments, coding platforms, physical computing kits, and AI tools — are selected and deployed based on their instructional fit with specific learning goals, with schools maintaining a coherent tool ecosystem across grade levels.
  • Schools make deliberate, transparent, and revisable decisions about student access to AI tools, grounded in their community's values, students' developmental levels, and an honest assessment of both the learning opportunities and the risks these tools present.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Equitable access to devices and connectivity is guaranteed.
A 1:1 device-to-student ratio with reliable WiFi at school and home. Device refresh schedules prevent equity gaps from widening over time as hardware ages.
Technology procurement is aligned to curriculum.
All hardware and software purchases directly support curriculum requirements and are sustainable beyond individual teachers. AI platforms and software are evaluated for long-term viability — not just grant-period coverage.
Systems manage technology reliably.
An LMS organizes coursework, clear procedures govern device distribution and security, and digital citizenship policies are in place. Tech support with clear troubleshooting protocols serves students, families, and staff.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
AI educational tools proliferate faster than schools can evaluate them. Without quality control frameworks, schools risk adopting tools that are pedagogically weak, inequitable, or unsustainable.
Design response
Use the Curriculum Evaluator Tool and AI Curriculum Airtable to assess tools against criteria including alignment to Computational Literacy outcomes, equity features, and evidence of effectiveness.
Space & Facilities
How physical learning environments are designed, arranged, and equipped to support the full range of Computational Literacy learning modes.
Goals for this design
  • Every student has equitable access to the devices, connectivity, software, and physical materials required to participate fully in Computational Literacy learning.
  • The physical classroom environment is intentionally configured to support the mode of computing work students are doing, with spaces that flex between collaborative design, individual coding, hardware building, and whole-class discussion.
  • Computing tools are selected and deployed based on their instructional fit with specific learning goals, with schools maintaining a coherent tool ecosystem so students build genuine fluency rather than constantly adapting to new interfaces.
  • Schools make deliberate, transparent, and revisable decisions about physical space configuration, grounded in the full range of learning modes Computational Literacy instruction requires.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Spaces support the full range of Computational Literacy learning modes.
Effective computing spaces accommodate pair programming, group projects, design sprints, debugging sessions, hardware building, and whole-class discussion. A traditional fixed-row computer lab serves none of these well.
Physical computing materials are treated as core infrastructure.
Robotics kits, microcontrollers, sensors, and maker tools are budgeted on the same cycle as textbooks, with assigned maintenance responsibility and documented checkout systems.
Flexible furniture enables multiple configurations.
Modular furniture reconfigurable in under two minutes, power access across the room, large shared displays visible from all positions, adequate material storage, and writable surfaces for collaborative design.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Computing educators often inherit spaces not designed for their pedagogy. Making the case for reconfiguration requires explaining why a "computer lab" is insufficient.
Design response
Audit existing spaces against the full range of Computational Literacy learning modes. Use the audit to generate specific renovation requests. Spatial disparities are a learning equity issue.
Family & Community Partnerships
How schools engage caregivers and communities as partners in students' computing and AI learning.
Goals for this element
  • Families are informed early, often, and specifically about what AI is being used in class, why, and what student data it touches — recognizing that as of 2025, 96% of families with elementary-aged children reported receiving no AI-related communication from their school.
  • Communication about CS and AI instruction is bidirectional and ongoing — allowing families to ask questions and share concerns as tools evolve.
  • Families are meaningfully engaged as partners in their children's computing and AI learning — with opportunities to understand what students are doing, why it matters, and how to support learning at home.
  • Engagement practices are designed to reach all families, including those facing language barriers, schedule constraints, limited digital access, or historical marginalization from school systems.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Families are partners, not recipients of information.
Computational Literacy nights, workshops on supporting computational thinking at home, and opportunities to participate in showcases position caregivers as active participants. Their expertise about their children and communities informs the design.
Real-world learning connects students to community and careers.
Guest speakers, workplace tours, student mentorships, and community-based projects connect students to Computational Literacy applications and career pathways — making learning relevant and broadening participation.
AI communication is proactive and transparent.
Schools provide regular updates when new tools are introduced, explain what data is collected and how it is protected, and offer ready-made communication templates so messaging quality doesn't depend on individual teacher initiative.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Many caregivers have limited technical backgrounds and may experience AI anxiety — which can translate into skepticism about classroom practices if schools don't address it directly.
Design response
Offer adult-only sessions where caregivers experience CS and AI activities themselves. Acknowledge uncertainty openly — families who feel included in the learning process trust schools more than those who feel information is being managed.
Budget & Operations
How resources are allocated, sustained, and diversified to support a durable Computational Literacy program.
Goals for this design
  • Computational Literacy education is funded as a core subject, with budget allocations for curriculum, professional learning, coaching, hardware, and AI tools that reflect the instructional priorities of the school's design.
  • Implementation spending matches or exceeds adoption spending — the cost of acquiring curriculum materials and tools is only the beginning; sustainable improvement requires ongoing investment in professional learning, coaching, and hardware refresh cycles.
  • AI tool procurement is governed by a deliberate vetting framework that evaluates instructional alignment, evidence of impact, student data privacy, equity of access, and total cost of ownership.
  • Operational systems — scheduling, procurement timelines, staffing allocation, and infrastructure planning — are designed to enable the Computational Literacy instructional model rather than constrain it.
  • Resource allocation in CS and AI education actively counteracts historical inequities: schools serving students most systematically excluded from computer science receive the instructional resources, qualified staffing, and operational support needed to provide rigorous, accessible learning.
  • Temporary funding opportunities are used strategically to build infrastructure and initiate programs, with transition plans that identify how high-leverage investments will be sustained through recurring budget lines when one-time funds expire.
What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Computational Literacy essentials are funded sustainably.
Budgets cover technology, staff, professional development, curriculum, and infrastructure — annually, not just in year one. Initial and ongoing costs are planned explicitly rather than assumed to decrease.
AI tool funding extends beyond grant periods.
New AI platforms and software are evaluated for sustainability. Schools avoid building dependence on short-term funding that disappears in 2–3 years, displacing both tools and the teacher capacity built around them.
Staffing resources are allocated strategically.
Dedicated Computational Literacy educators, specialists across grade levels, planning time for integration, instructional coaches, and coordinators for secondary pathways are funded as core positions — not add-ons.
Funding sources are diverse.
Local partnerships, state and federal grants (including NSF and USDOE investments in AI education), and community fundraising supplement core allocations — reducing vulnerability to any single funding source.
Continuous Learning & Improvement
The systems and routines used to define success, measure progress, and reflect together to improve quality and impact.
Goals for this design
  • Improvement efforts in Computational Literacy education are sustained, focused, and cumulative — with schools selecting a small number of high-leverage instructional priorities and pursuing them over multiple years rather than cycling through disconnected initiatives.
  • Assessment practices in Computational Literacy classrooms serve instructional purposes — providing timely, actionable feedback to both students and teachers about conceptual understanding, computational thinking, and coding proficiency.
  • Collaborative data structures and protocols support Computational Literacy teachers in examining student work and thinking together, breaking the professional isolation that characterizes CS teaching in most schools.
  • Implementation monitoring in Computational Literacy education is framed as a learning and support practice — using content-specific look-fors and observation tools that help leaders identify where teachers need content knowledge development or coaching.
  • Equity is an explicit and ongoing object of continuous improvement — using participation and outcomes data to identify and address structural barriers, and embedding equity review into every data cycle.
  • CS and AI improvement efforts are connected to broader professional networks and research-practice partnerships that can supply curriculum-aligned assessments, implementation tools, and cross-school learning.

What it looks like in a strong Computational Literacy design
Data systems are aligned to Computational Literacy learning outcomes.
LMS and data review processes track Computational Literacy learning outcomes, culture, perception, and student engagement — not just usage metrics.
Evaluation and adaptation happen regularly.
Computational Literacy classroom observations, data review meetings, and surveys monitor implementation fidelity, workload, and educator mindsets. Findings drive adjustments, not just documentation.
AI tool effectiveness is continuously evaluated.
How AI platforms and tools are used in instruction, their impact on student learning and educator practice, and stakeholder feedback on AI integration all inform ongoing decisions.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Assessment instruments for CS and AI literacy are still in development. Districts often evaluate AI education through usage metrics rather than learning outcomes.
Design response
Develop curriculum-based assessments rather than waiting for standardized tests. Use student work, debugging logs, and project portfolios as evidence of learning. Connect to research-practice partnerships developing better instruments.

Creo College Prep

A charter middle school where CS is a core subject — not an elective — for every student in grades 5–8. Students follow a four-year scaffolded pathway from foundational digital skills through programming and physical computing, taught by a dedicated CS educator at each grade level. New York City's first Career and Technical Education (CTE) charter high school, making computer science a core subject for all four years. K–12 Network · Grades PK–12
Harmony Public Schools
Texas (61 campuses) · 3,111 students (4 Austin campuses) · 67% Free & Reduced Lunch

A 61-school STEM-focused public charter network serving students across Texas. All students in K–8 participate in structured CS and computational thinking as a core subject. In high school, students choose from ten CTE pathways — four of which include computational literacy content — with opportunities for AP CS and industry certifications. Industry partners include AMD, Amazon, Samsung, and UT Austin.

K–12 Network · Grades K–12
RePublic Schools
Jackson, Mississippi & Nashville, Tennessee · 2,970 students · 69% Free & Reduced Lunch

A seven-school charter network providing computer science as a standalone subject to every student from kindergarten through 10th grade. Students progress from playful, hands-on computational thinking in elementary school to app-building and data work in middle school — then choose between AP CS, industry preparation, or an arts-focused pathway in high school. Partnerships with Blacks in Technology and Nashville Software School explicitly connect learning to real-world careers.
